## Why a checklist helps

Beginners often struggle because the case feels wide open. A checklist does not replace judgment, but it gives you a stable sequence so you do not skip obvious context or panic when the signal looks messy.

## First-pass checklist

1. Confirm what triggered the review.
2. Check the core account details and recent changes.
3. Review the most relevant recent activity.
4. Identify what looks inconsistent or unusual.
5. Decide whether the safest next step is clear, monitor, or escalate.

## Questions to ask at each step

### Trigger

- What exactly made this case suspicious?
- Is the signal behavioral, transactional, or account-access related?

### Account context

- Has anything changed recently on the account?
- Does the profile itself look incomplete or unusual?

### Activity review

- Are there repeated actions, sudden spikes, or timing anomalies?
- Does the recent pattern look like normal use for this account?

### Decision

- Do I have enough evidence to explain the behavior?
- What remains uncertain?
- What action is the safest and most defensible?

## What not to do

- Do not assume the first suspicious detail explains everything.
- Do not write a conclusion before you review the surrounding context.
- Do not escalate just to avoid thinking.
- Do not clear a case simply because you are uncomfortable with uncertainty.

## What a good beginner note sounds like

A good note is usually short, concrete, and structured. It says what you looked at, what stood out, and why the next step makes sense.

> Strong investigation writing is usually calm and specific, not dramatic.

## Use the checklist until the structure becomes natural

Over time, experienced analysts internalize this flow. Early on, it helps to keep the steps visible and repeatable so every case becomes a training loop instead of a guessing exercise.
