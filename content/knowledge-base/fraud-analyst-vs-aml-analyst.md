## Why people mix these roles together

Both roles investigate unusual behavior, review alerts, and document decisions. From the outside, they can look almost identical because both involve pattern recognition, case review, and escalation handling.

## What fraud analysts usually focus on

Fraud analysts are commonly concerned with direct misuse or abuse of an account, payment instrument, or digital channel.

- Account takeover
- Friendly fraud or chargeback abuse
- Suspicious payment behavior
- Device or identity inconsistencies
- Behavior that suggests immediate customer or business loss

## What AML analysts usually focus on

AML analysts are more often focused on whether activity suggests laundering behavior, suspicious flow of funds, or regulatory risk.

- Unusual transaction patterns
- Structuring or layered movement of funds
- Customer activity inconsistent with expected profile
- Cases that may require escalation for suspicious activity review

## The practical difference

Fraud work often asks, **is someone abusing this account or payment flow right now?**

AML work more often asks, **does this activity suggest suspicious movement of money that needs deeper compliance review or escalation?**

The workflows overlap, but the main risk question is not exactly the same.

## Shared skills

- Reviewing alerts quickly but carefully
- Explaining conclusions clearly
- Comparing behavior against expected customer context
- Escalating when evidence is incomplete
- Staying consistent under pressure

## Beginner advice

Do not worry too much about choosing the perfect specialty on day one. A lot of the core investigation habits overlap.

What matters first is whether you can:

1. Identify why a case looks unusual.
2. Pull the right context around that signal.
3. Explain a reasonable next step.

## A simple way to think about it

If the question is mainly about misuse, abuse, or immediate customer loss, the work often feels more fraud-oriented.

If the question is mainly about suspicious flow of funds, compliance risk, or escalation for regulatory review, the work often feels more AML-oriented.
